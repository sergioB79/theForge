SUBTITLE: Scales Without Weight

Rating systems deserve a clear gaze and a steady hand: they touch value, influence behavior, and sometimes wound, but as systems they rarely submit themselves to the same exposed discipline they impose on others. They are important, yes, but importance alone is not enough for admission into the Forge.

‚ùå Why It Fails the Forge:
- **Structural failure**  
  Rating systems rarely *require* human presence in any thick sense; they are optimized for remote, low-friction interaction. The rite is a click or a number, dislocated from context. Their operation presumes ideal abstraction: that a lived experience can be compressed into stars or scores and still mean something stable. When real conditions intrude‚Äîbias, fatigue, manipulation‚Äîthe structure compensates with more data, not more presence. They simulate rigor through quantification, but their core mechanism is mechanical, not embodied.

- **Ethical failure**  
  The consequence of a rating usually flows one way: toward the rated, not the rater. The one who judges is protected by anonymity, scale, and platform design; they can act without durable cost or reflection. This asymmetry undermines any claim to rite: a true rite risks the practitioner as much as the subject. Rating systems externalize moral weight while masking responsibility in distributions and averages. They enforce norms without admitting their own situatedness or offering reciprocal vulnerability.

- **Symbolic failure**  
  Symbols in the Forge must thicken perception; rating systems thin it. They encourage substitution of the symbol (the score) for the thing itself, teaching users to navigate a world of aggregates instead of encounters. What looks like a shared language of value is mostly a shorthand for convenience. Failure modes‚Äîmanipulated reviews, skewed incentives, reputation inflation‚Äîoften remain invisible to the casual participant, absorbed into opaque algorithms and hidden moderation. Where a rite makes failure legible and instructive, ratings tend to blur it into noise, preserving the mechanism while sacrificing understanding.

‚ùå Verdict:
Rating systems are powerful instruments of coordination and control, but they do not rise to the level of a shared rite that exposes both actor and structure to visible consequence.
